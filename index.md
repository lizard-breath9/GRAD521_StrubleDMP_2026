# Data Description
This project focuses on using inoculated soil seed coatings to improve plant establishment and
ameliorate extreme weather stress for two commonly used restoration species, Sandberg
bluegrass (Poa secunda) and bottlebrush squirreltail (Elymus elymoides). Project sites will be at
six locations across five states (Oregon, Washington, Idaho, Wyoming, and Nevada). Our overall
objective is to improve understanding of soil microbial variation, the role of soil microbes in
plant establishment and growth, and the potential usage of inoculated soil seed coatings. Soil
microbes can provide unique benefits to the seedlings they associate with, and our goal is to
explore these relationships. 

Our research questions are as follows: 1. What role do climate and
plant community composition play in influencing soil microbial composition?, 2. How does the
soil microbial composition influence plant emergence, growth, and survival of native and
invasive species?, and 3. Can we use inoculated soil seed coatings to improve restoration
outcomes in cheatgrass-invaded areas? 
Specifically, our objectives are: 1. quantify how
microbial communities differ between natives and invasives across multiple states, 2. determine
how microbial soil coatings affect germination, growth, and survival, 3. examine the
effectiveness of seed coatings planted in invasive soils, and 4. test the ability of microbial
coatings to ameliorate stress under drought and heatwave. 
This project is in the field of rangeland ecology, and I will be involved in every step of the project from conception to data collection and analysis to publication, as it is my master’s thesis research.

For objective 1, we will be collecting soil from native and invasive plant communities at each of
the six sites. We will identify communities of the two native plants referenced above and an
additional community of cheatgrass (an invasive plant), and collect soil from near the roots of
these species using a soil corer. After this, we will extract soil DNA from each of the soils and
send it for sequencing.

For objective 2, we will run an experiment on a thermogradient table (which captures a full
factorial day/night temperature regime). Seeds of each of the three plants listed above will be
planted in a clear agar (as opposed to soil so we can see root growth). The seeds will be coated in
either a native soil (6), an invasive soil (7), or a control (no soil), and we will measure
emergence, root growth, and shoot growth for two weeks.

For objective 3, we will run another experiment in a greenhouse, and we will use only the two
native species. The native seeds will be coated in native soil only from each site (6) and planted
into either cheatgrass soil (7) or a control soil (we only coat the seeds in the native soil, as this is
the practical, restoration application). We will measure emergence, shoot growth, height, and
final biomass and survival for 12 weeks.

For objective 4, we will do the same experiment as objective 3, but at week 4, we will apply
either a drought, heatwave, drought+heatwave, or no drought or heatwave (control conditions),
and measure the same plant traits.

List of all the data:
1. Soils: six native soils and six invasive soils
a. Physical samples (no gigabytes of data)
b. Spreadsheets with site location (coordinates), time, date, nearby plant community,
and sample ID (~50 kilobytes)
2. DNA samples: soil DNA from each of the soil samples
a. Physical samples (no gigabytes of data)
b. Spreadsheets with the same information as soils (~50 kilobytes)
3. DNA sequences
a. Text files (~2.0 megabytes)
4. Thermogradient table study: seed emergence, root growth, and shoot growth
a. Physical samples (while the study is conducted, no gigabytes of data)
b. Spreadsheets with sample ID, site, species, treatment (native soil, invasive soil, or
no soil), temperature (day/night), emergence, root growth, and shoot growth - data
collected daily for two weeks (~600 kilobytes)
5. Greenhouse study: seed emergence, height, shoot growth, final biomass, final survival
a. Physical samples (while the study is conducted, no gigabytes of data)
b. Spreadsheets with sample ID, site, species, soil treatment (cheatgrass soil or
control soil), weather treatment (drought, heatwave, drought+heatwave, control),
emergence, plant height, shoot growth, survival, and final biomass - data collected
two to three times a week for twelve weeks (~600 kilobytes)

For this research project, data management roles will be shared among three to four members of
the Martyn lab: Dr. Trace Martyn (PI), Elizabeth Struble (master’s student), and any
undergraduate interns (1-2 per summer, with potential for further involvement). This document
will outline the various roles and responsibilities of each member, where the data will be stored,
any special requirements, and back-up data management. Should any member of the project be
unable to complete their role, Struble will complete their responsibilities until the lab decides on
a permanent replacement. If Struble is unable to do so, she will coordinate with Dr. Martyn on
either sharing responsibilities or finding another lab member or station employee who can assist

Data Manager: Elizabeth Struble

Struble will be the main manager of any data collected. She will be the primary owner of
any Excel or other online data storage spreadsheets and will be accountable for any paper
data collection documents. She will ensure undergraduate interns enter any data collected
on paper into Excel or similar documents in a timely manner (within two weeks of data
collection, or another agreed-upon deadline). Struble will also manage a lab folder to
hold any data collection sheets and make photo copies for data preservation.

Protection of sensitive and protected data: Dr. Martyn

There are no plans to collect sensitive or protected data, but should the need arise, project
members will coordinate with Dr. Martyn to ensure data is stored in the proper places.

Instrumentation maintenance: Elizabeth Struble and Undergraduate Interns

Struble will perform routine checks for the quality and proper functioning of any lab or
data collection materials. This will include (but is not limited to): TDR probe, soil corer,
infrared heaters, watering systems, and inspecting the greenhouse and lab space. Should
any problems arise, Struble will either trouble shoot the problem, or contact the necessary
authority on the instrument (such in the case with the watering system or TDR probe).
Undergraduate interns will be trained in proper inspection and maintenance of lab and
field equipment as well. As part of their experience, they will help with monitoring the
greenhouse and other equipment, and should any issues arise, contact either Struble or
Dr. Martyn to resolve the issue (if they cannot do so themselves).

Data collection/data generation: Dr. Martyn, Elizabeth Struble, and Undergraduate
Interns

All members of the project are expected to contribute to data collection. Undergraduate
interns will be trained by Dr. Martyn and Struble on the proper field collection protocol

Data organization: Elizabeth Struble and Undergraduate Interns

format for the data collection Excel sheets and will check both physical and digital data

on.

Data analysis: Dr. Martyn and Elizabeth Struble

Struble will conduct the majority of the data analysis, mostly in R Studio. As this project

# Storage and Security

This project will have both physical copies of datasheets and online data spreadsheets. In total,
there will be five copies of the data (two physical and three digital).

1. Physical datasheets:
Any datasheets must be written in pen for future legibility (pencil will smudge away).
Any mistakes should be marked out with a single line through the mistake, and the
correction written to the side (make note of the mistake in the notes section, if one is
present). There will be separate lab folders for each component of the project
(thermogradient and greenhouse), where the original copies will be stored. Every two
weeks (one thermogradient table run), Struble will check to see if all datasheets have
been stored, and make photocopies of the datasheets, which will be stored in another
overall project folder. Every four weeks, the same will be done with the greenhouse data
(less data is collected for the greenhouse, so we do not need to check as often). If
undergraduate interns are using the datasheets for data entry or other purposes, that is
okay, and Struble will make copies after the interns are finished with the documents.

3. Online Datasheets:
Excel will be the primary data entry tool. There will be three separate Excel sheets (soil
collection, thermogradient, and greenhouse). Struble will be responsible for entering data
into the soil collection sheet, and undergraduate interns will be responsible for data entry
in the latter two (Struble will have ownership of the documents, though). Each sheet will
have at least two tabs, one for metadata and another for the data. The spreadsheets will be
uploaded to the cloud as a backup. Struble will also download the latest copy of the
datasheets every two weeks. As there will be physical copies of the data, there is less
concern about potentially losing data, so downloads do not need to be frequent.
After Struble graduates, she will transfer ownership to Dr. Martyn for future use of the
data by the Martyn lab.

If both the physical sheets and online versions of the data are lost, each member of the lab will be
contacted to see if they have a version of the data downloaded to their personal device that they
would be willing to share. We hope that with these multiple copies of the data (both physical and
online), this will not occur. As our data collection cannot be easily redone, if there are no copies
of the data, the project will proceed without the data and will be noted in any documents and
publications.
