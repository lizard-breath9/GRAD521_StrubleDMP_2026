# GRAD521_StrubleDMP_2026

##Description of project: 
1. This project focuses on using inoculated soil seed coatings to improve plant establishment and ameliorate extreme weather stress for two commonly used restoration species, Sandberg bluegrass (Poa secunda) and bottlebrush squirreltail (Elymus elymoides). Project sites will be at six locations across five states (Oregon, Washington, Idaho, Wyoming, and Nevada). Our overall objective is to improve understanding of soil microbial variation, the role of soil microbes in plant establishment and growth, and the potential usage of inoculated soil seed coatings. Soil microbes can provide unique benefits to the seedlings they associate with, and our goal is to explore these relationships.

Our research questions are as follows: 
1. What role do climate and plant community composition play in influencing soil microbial composition?,
2. How does the soil microbial composition influence plant emergence, growth, and survival of native and invasive species?, and
3. Can we use inoculated soil seed coatings to improve restoration outcomes in cheatgrass-invaded areas?

Specifically, our objectives are:
1. quantify how microbial communities differ between natives and invasives across multiple states,
2. determine how microbial soil coatings affect germination, growth, and survival,
3. examine the effectiveness of seed coatings planted in invasive soils, and
4. test the ability of microbial coatings to ameliorate stress under drought and heatwave.

This project is in the field of rangeland ecology, and I will be involved in every step of the project from conception to data collection and analysis to publication, as it is my master’s thesis research.

For objective 1, we will be collecting soil from native and invasive plant communities at each of the six sites. We will identify communities of the two native plants referenced above and an additional community of cheatgrass (an invasive plant), and collect soil from near the roots of these species using a soil corer. After this, we will extract soil DNA from each of the soils and send it for sequencing.

For objective 2, we will run an experiment on a thermogradient table (which captures a full factorial day/night temperature regime). Seeds of each of the three plants listed above will be planted in a clear agar (as opposed to soil so we can see root growth). The seeds will be coated in either a native soil (6), an invasive soil (7), or a control (no soil), and we will measure emergence, root growth, and shoot growth for two weeks.

For objective 3, we will run another experiment in a greenhouse, and we will use only the two native species. The native seeds will be coated in native soil only from each site (6) and planted into either cheatgrass soil (7) or a control soil (we only coat the seeds in the native soil, as this is the practical, restoration application). We will measure emergence, shoot growth, height, and final biomass and survival for 12 weeks.

For objective 4, we will do the same experiment as objective 3, but at week 4, we will apply either a drought, heatwave, drought+heatwave, or no drought or heatwave (control conditions), and measure the same plant traits.

List of all the data:
1. Soils: six native soils and six invasive soils
  a. Physical samples (no gigabytes of data)
  b. Spreadsheets with site location (coordinates), time, date, nearby plant community, and sample ID (~50 kilobytes)
2. DNA samples: soil DNA from each of the soil samples
  a. Physical samples (no gigabytes of data)
  b. Spreadsheets with the same information as soils (~50 kilobytes)
3. DNA sequences
  a. Text files (~2.0 megabytes)
4. Thermogradient table study: seed emergence, root growth, and shoot growth
  a. Physical samples (while the study is conducted, no gigabytes of data)
  b. Spreadsheets with sample ID, site, species, treatment (native soil, invasive soil, or no soil), temperature (day/night), emergence, root growth, and shoot growth - data collected daily for two weeks (~600 kilobytes)
5. Greenhouse study: seed emergence, height, shoot growth, final biomass, final survival
  a. Physical samples (while the study is conducted, no gigabytes of data)
  b. Spreadsheets with sample ID, site, species, soil treatment (cheatgrass soil or control soil), weather treatment (drought, heatwave, drought+heatwave, control), emergence, plant height, shoot growth, survival, and final biomass - data collected two to three times a week for twelve weeks (~600 kilobytes)
